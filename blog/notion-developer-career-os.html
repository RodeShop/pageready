Why Developers Need a Career System

You have 5 side projects on GitHub. 3 half-finished courses. A skills list that's never updated. And a networking list that's just names with no context.

This is how most developers manage their career: scattered across GitHub repos, browser bookmarks, and mental notes. You know you should be tracking your growth, but there's no system that connects what you're building, what you're learning, and who you know.

The problem isn't that you don't care about your career — it's that the tools we use for career tracking are fundamentally disconnected. GitHub shows your projects. Your browser shows your courses. Your LinkedIn shows your network. And your memory? It's terrible at remembering which skill you practiced last month.

Introducing the Developer Career OS

The Developer Career OS is a Notion template built for software developers who want to track their career growth systematically. It connects four databases — Projects, Skills, Learning, and Networking — so you can see the full picture of your career in one place.

Every project links to skills. Every learning item links to a skill. Every networking contact has context and a last-contact date. When you open the template, you don't just see a list — you see the story of your career.

What's Inside

Projects Database

Your side projects, open source contributions, freelance work, and learning projects. Track the tech stack, GitHub URL, live URL, and stars. The Skill Count rollup shows how many skills each project uses.

Skills Database

Your technical and soft skills. Track the level (Beginner → Expert), category (Frontend, Backend, DevOps, etc.), last used date, and hours practiced. Link each skill to the projects that use it and the learning that builds it.

Learning Database

Courses, books, tutorials, and conferences. Track status (Not Started → In Progress → Completed), hours spent, and quality ratings. Link each learning item to the skill it builds.

Networking Database

Mentors, peers, recruiters, and industry contacts. Track the relationship type, platform, last contact date, and how you met. Use the Last Contact field to schedule follow-ups.

How It All Connects

The magic is in the connections. When you add a project and link it to skills, you can see which skills each project builds. When you add a learning item and link it to a skill, you can track your investment in each area. When you update the Hours Practiced on a skill, you see your growth over time.

This isn't a template with four separate databases. It's one system where every piece of your career tells a story.

FAQ

Q: Can I use this for non-technical skills?

A: Yes. The "Soft Skill" category is designed for communication, leadership, system design, etc.

Q: How do I track open source contributions?

A: Create a Project with Type = "Open Source" and link the GitHub repo. Track stars over time.

Q: Can I use this for job applications?

A: Yes. The Projects database is your portfolio. Reference it in cover letters and interviews.
